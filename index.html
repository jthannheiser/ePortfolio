    Project Idea

    Overview

    From the start of this year till the end, the COVID-19 pandemic has remained as a constant throughout. A specific service that has been heavily affected are restaurants and cafes and because of social distancing requirements, there are limits on how many people can be in one establishment based on the relative size of the building. My project idea is a smartphone app to check how many people there may be at a particular restaurant or café, provide the user with an estimated wait time based on this data, and provide a way to virtually queue with the use of QR codes.

    Motivation

    If you have recently gone out to a café to sit down and have a meal, depending on the popularity of the place visited, you may have been told there was a wait time and asked to provide your name and number to be contacted when space is available. For some people, this could be a deciding factor in choosing where to eat. My app aims to assist people by showing approximate wait times and providing an easy way to queue for places that do not take bookings. With over 8000 cafes in Australia alone (Core List, 2020) patrons could enjoy a more hassle-free experience when choosing where to have coffee with the use of this app.

    Description

    The name decided for the app is Waitr and as previously mentioned, it will provide a virtual queuing system as well as an estimated wait time. There will be two sections for the app, with the main components of the app being for the users and the section for business owners acting more behind-the-scenes. Upon first launch, users will be prompted to create an account using either their mobile number or email address, which will then send an account verification message to prevent the creation of spam accounts. Restaurants and cafes will have to opt in to use Waitr as a QR code is required on the premises to get into the queue. Account verification for business owners is crucial and may have to manually verified.

    The UI will consist of a slide out menu with access to account settings, recently visited, and a map showing restaurants/cafes near you with each having either a red, yellow, or green symbol showing how busy they are – red being the busiest and green being the least. Once the user reaches a restaurant, there could be a photo in the app showing where the QR code is located so they can scan into the queue. While in the virtual queue, the user can see what position they are in via the app itself. Ideally, there would also be a notification in the notification tray that is also present on the lock screen showing their position in the queue with push notifications letting the user know when they move up the queue.

    Waitr will also have a section to help improve the accuracy of the wait timer. If insufficient data can be collected from the Google Geolocation API, then the restaurant/cafe staff could assist in providing the wait time. Once they are let in, patrons could then confirm the accuracy of the wait time provided with the choice of three options: longer than expected, on time, or faster than expected. Once this choice is selected, the restaurant would have access to the user’s input to improve their estimate and the overall experience provided.

    To prevent attempts to get in the queue before the user reaches the establishment, location services could be paired with the use of the QR code. Alternatively, if there is an issue with the user’s device, staff could enter the user into the queue using either the user’s email or mobile number used when creating the account.

    Finally, there would be the ‘recently visited’ section which shows the places recently checked into in chronological order. Other than tracking the places visited, it may also provide an average wait time based on the user’s last visits. It could also serve as a personal rating system, with the user being able to add comments about their recent trips for future reference.

    Tools & Technologies

    The obvious hardware requirement would be a smart phone with location services enabled. Waitr could also utilise Google’s Geolocation API to provide an approximate number of people like it does on Google Maps. The user could also contribute to the accuracy of Waitr by either confirming the wait time was as anticipated or giving a lower/higher amount. The results of these user answers, as well as the users account data could then be stored on an online database tool provided by Amazon or Google. As previously mentioned, there would also be a QR code component that would hook into the app and place you in a queue that is managed and viewable by the restaurant.

    Skills Required

    The main software that would need to be written would be Waitr itself. Google’s Geolocation API utilises JSON as the data format, but it is ‘completely language independent’ (Introducing JSON, n.d.). The language JSON is based off is JavaScript which is an extremely popular language (Spinx Digital, 2020) and would be my choice when developing Waitr. The only programming language I have a basic understanding of is Java, so this may be out of reach at this current point in time.

    Outcome

    If successful, this project will provide a way for people to check restaurant and café wait times, virtually queue, and be alerted when it is their turn to be seated. It may cause people to check out less popular places when they are presented with a thirty-minute wait time or let them spend the time they would have waited in a queue more productively e.g., grocery shopping or going for a walk. If this project succeeds, it will be a considerable quality-of-life improvement with queue times being made clearer while also providing a way for business to easily manage patrons.
